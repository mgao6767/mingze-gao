Welcome to this workshop on assessing banks‚Äô capital adequacy. Our primary objective is to ensure you gain a comprehensive understanding of how capital ratios are determined taking into account both on-balance-sheet and off-balance-sheet activities. While the questions may seem straightforward, they are designed to effectively achieve this goal. Through a series of targeted questions and practical exercises, we aim to demystify the complexities of capital adequacy and provide you with the tools needed to apply these concepts effectively.

Recap

Under Basel III, depositary institutions (DIs) calculate and monitor four capital ratios:

  1. Common equity Tier 1 (CET1) risk-based capital ratio \[ \text{CET1 capital ratio} = \frac{\text{CET1 capital}}{\text{Risk-weighted assets}} \tag{1}\]

  2. Tier 1 risk-based capital ratio \[ \text{Tier 1 capital ratio} = \frac{\text{Tier 1 capital}}{\text{Risk-weighted assets}} \tag{2}\]

  3. Total risk-based capital ratio \[ \text{Total capital ratio} = \frac{\text{Total capital}}{\text{Risk-weighted assets}} \tag{3}\]

  4. Tier 1 leverage ratio \[ \text{Tier 1 leverage ratio} = \frac{\text{Tier 1 capital}}{\text{Total exposure}} \tag{4}\]

Question 1 - Appetizers

Suppose a bank has CET1 capital of $100 million, Tier 1 capital of $120 million, total capital of $200 million. The bank uses IRB and calculates its risk-weighted assets (RWA) to be $2,000 million.1 What are the bank‚Äôs CET1 capital ratio, Tier 1 capital ratio, and total capital ratio?

Given the calculated capital ratios, does the bank meet the minimum required capital adequacy ratios?

Question 2 - Capital buffers

Meeting the minimum required capital adequacy ratios is nothing to be proud of, because in practice there are also capital buffers applied.

Suppose now a Capital Conversion Buffer of 2.5% applies, while the Countercyclical Capital Buffer (CCyB) is lifted2, does the bank in Question 1 still have sufficient capital? If not, what would be the consequences?

Let‚Äôs now assess another bank that reports a CET1 capital ratio of 8%. With a Capital Conversion Buffer of 2.5%, does the bank have sufficient CET1 capital? What if there is also a CCyB of 1.5%?

Question 3 - RWA! RWA! RWA!

Important

Note that when calculating capital ratios in Equation 1, Equation 2, and Equation 3, the denominator RWA should be the sum of three components: RWA for credit risk, RWA for market risk, and RWA for operational risk.

In this course, we covered the calculation of RWA (for credit risk). This question aims to progressively examine your skills to compute RWA.
