Beta - Unlevered and Levered

Teaching Notes
Author
Affiliation

Mingze Gao, PhD

Macquarie University

Published

March 24, 2020

Beta is a measure of market risk. This post tries to explain the unlevered and levered betas.

Unlevered Firm u

If a firm has no debt, it‚Äôs all equity-financed and thus its equity‚Äôs beta \(\beta_{E}\) equals its asset‚Äôs beta \(\beta_{A}\). This beta is also the unlevered beta, \(\beta_{\text{unlevered}}\), since it‚Äôs unaffected by leverage. The unlevered beta measures the market risk exposure of the firm‚Äôs shareholders. Let‚Äôs call this firm \(u\), Hence, we have:

\[ \beta_{\text{unlevered}}=\beta_E^u=\beta_A^u \tag{1}\]

This equality says that in an unlevered firm, the unlevered beta equals its equity beta and its asset beta.

Levered Firm l

If the same firm is partly financed by debt, let‚Äôs call it firm \(l\). The asset of the levered firm \(l\) is financed by both equity and debt, and hence the asset‚Äôs market risk is from both equity and debt. The asset‚Äôs beta is a weighted average of its equity beta and debt beta.

\[ \beta_A^l = \frac{E}{E+D(1-t)} \beta_E^l + \frac{D(1-t)}{E+D(1-t)} \beta_D^l \tag{2}\]

Note

\(\beta_A^l\) measures the change in the return on a portfolio of all firm \(l\)‚Äôs securities (debt and equity) for each additional one percent change in the market return.

This part is not very hard to understand. The beta of a portfolio is the weighted average beta of its constituents. If you believe that debt beta is zero since the value of debt may not be affected by the equity market, then \(\beta_D^l=0\) and Equation 2 can be simplified to:

\[ \begin{align} \beta_A^l &= \frac{E}{E+D(1-t)} \beta_E^l \\ &= \frac{1}{1+\frac{D}{E}(1-t)} \beta_E^l \end{align} \tag{3}\]

However, this firm‚Äôs shareholders are now more exposed to the market risk than before, because leverage increases the variation in the payoff to shareholders. This means the equity‚Äôs beta of this levered firm is higher than the equity‚Äôs beta of the unlevered firm, i.e. \(\beta_E^l>\beta_E^u\).

Note that, the levered beta \(\beta_{\text{levered}}\) that we talk about refers to \(\beta_E^l\), which is the equity beta of the levered firm \(l\).

Unlevered vs Levered

On the other hand, firm \(u\) and firm \(l\) differ only in capital structure whilst both have the same asset. Let‚Äôs say we have a portfolio of firm \(u\)‚Äôs asset and the other portfolio of firm \(l\)‚Äôs asset, then these two portfolios should have the same expected return and market risk exposure.1 This means the two portfolios have the same beta, implying:

1 Modigliani-Miller theorem states that the capital structure should not affect a firm‚Äôs value.

\[\begin{equation}\beta_A^u = \beta_A^l \end{equation} \tag{4}\]

If we substitue in the definition of unlevered and levered beta (Equation 1 and Equation 3):

\[ \begin{equation} \beta_{\text{unlevered}} = \frac{1}{1+\frac{D}{E}(1-t)} \beta_{\text{levered}} \end{equation} \tag{5}\]

or

\[ \begin{equation} \beta_{\text{levered}} = \left( 1+\frac{D}{E}(1-t) \right) \beta_{\text{unlevered}} \end{equation} \tag{6}\]

This is the formula that we use to lever and unlever beta.2

2 This Equation 6 is also named Hamada Equation, where we assumed a zero debt beta. It draws on the Modigliani-Miller theorem on capital structure, and appeared in Prof. Robert Hamada‚Äôs paper ‚ÄúThe Effect of the Firm‚Äôs Capital Structure on the Systematic Risk of Common Stocks‚Äù in the Journal of Finance in 1972.

Further Clarification

The equity beta of a firm with debts is levered. To remove the impact of leverage on shareholders‚Äô market risk exposure, we need to unlever this beta in order to get the unlevered beta. This unlevered beta is also called the asset beta.

Note that the asset beta is a syncronym for unlevered beta. It is not, however, the asset‚Äôs beta \(\beta_A^l\) when the firm is leveraged. This convention is confusing indeed, so throughout this post, I‚Äôm using asset‚Äôs beta to refer to the beta of a portfolio of all securities (debt and equity) of the levered firm.

Notations

  • \(\beta_E^u\): the equity‚Äôs beta of the unlevered firm
  • \(\beta_A^u\): the asset‚Äôs beta of the unlevered firm
  • \(\beta_E^l\): the equity‚Äôs beta of the levered firm
  • \(\beta_D^l\): the debt‚Äôs beta of the levered firm
  • \(\beta_A^l\): the asset‚Äôs beta of the levered firm
  • \(D\): the size of the firm‚Äôs debt
  • \(E\): the size of the firm‚Äôs equity
  • \(t\): the tax rate
  • \(\beta_{\text{unleverd}}\): unlevered beta, the equity (asset) beta of the unlevered version of the firm
  • \(\beta_{\text{leverd}}\): levered beta, the equity beta of the levered version of the firm
